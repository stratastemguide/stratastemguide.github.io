The Scientific Method

"Science isn't just about lab coats and bubbling beakers; it’s a systematic way of thinking to keep us from fooling ourselves."

The Roadmap: Step-by-Step

01.
Observation:

Noticing something unusual or interesting in the world around you.

02.
Question:

Asking "Why?" or "How?" based on what you saw.

03.
Hypothesis:

Formulating an educated "If... then..." guess that can be tested.

04.
Experiment:

Designing a fair test to collect observable data.

05.
Analysis:

Reviewing the data. Did the results match your expectations?

06.
Conclusion:

Deciding whether to support or reject your original hypothesis.

07.
Communication:

Sharing your findings so others can verify or build upon them.

Case Study: The Maggot Mystery

In 1668, Francesco Redi challenged the idea of Spontaneous Generation—the belief that life just "appeared" from non-living matter.

The Problem: Where do maggots on rotting meat actually come from?

The Experiment: Redi used three jars with meat:

  • Jar 1 (Open): Flies could reach the meat.
  • Jar 2 (Sealed): No flies or air could enter.
  • Jar 3 (Gauze): Air entered, but flies were blocked.

The Observation: Maggots only appeared in Jar 1. In Jar 3, flies laid eggs on top of the gauze, but no maggots appeared on the meat.

The Conclusion: Maggots come from fly eggs, not the meat itself. Spontaneous generation was debunked.

Understanding Variables

To keep an experiment "fair," you must manage the "ingredients" of your test.

Independent Variable

The "Cause"

The one thing you change on purpose to see what happens.

Ex: Amount of water given to a plant.

Dependent Variable

The "Effect"

The thing you measure. It changes based on the independent variable.

Ex: The height of the plant.

Controlled Variables

The "Constants"

Things you keep exactly the same so they don't interfere.

Ex: Same soil, same sunlight, same pot.
Rule of Thumb: "I change the Independent variable to see what happens to the Dependent variable, while keeping everything else Controlled."

The Control Experiment

A Control Experiment is your "Baseline." It is the version of your test where you don't apply the change. In Redi's experiment, the Open Jar was the control. It shows what happens under normal conditions so you have something to compare your results against.
