Non-Mendelian Inheritance

Back to Genetics Back to Path

In previous chapters, you learned how Gregor Mendel proposed his theory of inheritance, built upon three laws: the Law of Dominance, the Law of Segregation, and the Law of Independent Assortment.

Non-Mendelian inheritance refers to patterns of inheritance that do not follow Mendel’s rules, particularly the simple dominant-recessive relationship. These patterns reveal the complex ways genes interact to determine phenotypes.

1. Incomplete Dominance

In incomplete dominance, the heterozygous genotype results in a mixed phenotype that is a blend of the two homozygous phenotypes. Neither allele is fully dominant.

Example: Flower Color

  • Homozygous Dominant (RR): Red
  • Homozygous Recessive (rr): White
  • Heterozygous (Rr): Pink (A blend of red and white)

[Image: Incomplete Dominance - Red x White = Pink Flowers]

2. Codominance

Codominance occurs when both alleles of a gene are fully and simultaneously expressed in a heterozygous individual. Instead of blending, both traits appear clearly.

Example: Speckled Chickens

  • Black Feathers ($C^B C^B$) x White Feathers ($C^W C^W$)
  • Heterozygous Result ($C^B C^W$): Black and White Speckled feathers (Both colors are expressed).

[Image: Codominance - Black and White speckled chicken]

3. Multiple Alleles

Sometimes a single gene has more than two alternative alleles in a population. This is known as Polyallelism. While the population has multiple alleles, an individual can still only carry two (one from each parent).

Example: Human Blood Groups

Controlled by three alleles: $I^A$, $I^B$, and $i$. $I^A$ and $I^B$ are codominant, while both are dominant over $i$.

4. Polygenic Inheritance

A trait is controlled by two or more genes, often on different chromosomes. These traits show continuous variation rather than discrete categories.

  • Continuous Variation: Traits like height, skin color, and weight.
  • Quantitative Inheritance: The effect of each gene is additive.

5. Epistasis

Epistasis occurs when one gene masks or modifies the effect of another gene. This deviates from Mendel's simple ratios.

Recessive Epistasis

Homozygous recessive alleles of the "epistatic" gene mask the other gene (e.g., Albinism masking coat color).

Dominant Epistasis

A single dominant allele of the epistatic gene masks the expression of another gene.

[Images: Epistasis examples in fruit color and animal coats]

6. Pleiotropy

Pleiotropy is when a single gene influences multiple, seemingly unrelated traits. This is common in genetic disorders where one mutation affects multiple organ systems.

  • Sickle Cell Anemia: One gene mutation affects blood cell shape, organ health, and pain levels.
  • Marfan Syndrome: Affects stature, limbs, cardiovascular health, and vision.

7. Sex-Linked Inheritance

Traits controlled by genes located on the sex chromosomes (X or Y). Because males (XY) and females (XX) have different combinations, the inheritance patterns differ.

Example: Hemophilia

Located on the X chromosome. Males are more likely to be affected because they only have one X chromosome—one recessive allele is enough to cause the disorder.

[Image: Punnett square for sex-linked hemophilia]

8. Mitochondrial Inheritance

Mitochondria contain their own circular DNA. Because the egg provides almost all the cytoplasm and organelles to the zygote, mitochondria are inherited exclusively from the mother.

  • If a mother has a mitochondrial mutation, all her children will inherit it.
  • If a father has a mutation, none of his children will inherit it.
