Conservation of Energy

Back to Physics Back to Path

The Core Concept: Energy is a Shape-Shifter

Energy cannot be created from nothing, and it cannot be deleted from existence. The Law of Conservation of Energy states that the total energy of an isolated system remains constant. It simply changes form—moving from a "stored" state to an "active" state.

The Bank Account Analogy

Think of it like a bank account: you can move money from savings to checking, or withdraw it as cash (change forms), but the total amount of wealth remains the same unless you deposit or spend it outside the system.

The Primary Players

Before we look at the conservation itself, we need to define the two most common forms of mechanical energy you'll encounter in an isolated system.

1. Gravitational Potential Energy ($U_g$)

This is energy stored due to an object's position in a gravitational field. If it has the "potential" to fall, it has GPE.

$U_g = mgh$

  • m: mass (kg)
  • g: gravitational acceleration ($\approx 9.8 \text{ m s}^{-2}$)
  • h: height above a reference point (m)

2. Kinetic Energy ($K$)

This is the energy of motion. If it’s moving, it has Kinetic Energy ($K$).

$K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$

  • m: mass (kg)
  • v: velocity (m/s)

The Master Equation

In a "perfect" system (ignoring friction or air resistance), the sum of potential and kinetic energy at the start must equal the sum at the end. We call this Mechanical Energy ($E_{mech}$), the sum of both kinetic and potential energy.

$U_{initial} + K_{initial} = U_{final} + K_{final}$

Sample Problem: The Cliff Diver

Scenario: A diver with a mass of 70 kg stands motionless on a cliff that is 20 m above the water surface. He jumps off the cliff. Determine his velocity just before he reaches the water. (Assume $g = 10 \text{ m s}^{-2}$, and neglect air resistance.)

Step 1: Initial State (Top of the Cliff)

Height ($h$): $20 \text{ m}$, Velocity ($v$): $0 \text{ m s}^{-1}$

$U_i = mgh = 70 \times 10 \times 20 = \mathbf{14,000 \text{ J}}$

$K_i = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 70 \times 0^2 = \mathbf{0 \text{ J}}$

Total initial mechanical energy: $E_i = 14,000 \text{ J}$

Step 2: Final State (Just Above the Water)

Height ($h$): $0 \text{ m}$, Velocity ($v$): Unknown

$U_f = 0 \text{ J}$ (Height is zero)

$K_f = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 70 \times v^2 = \mathbf{35v^2 \text{ J}}$

Step 3: Apply Conservation of Mechanical Energy

$E_i = E_f$

$14,000 = 35v^2$

Step 4: Solve for Velocity

$v^2 = \frac{14,000}{35} = 400$

$v = \sqrt{400} = \mathbf{20 \text{ m s}^{-1}}$

Final Answer: $20 \text{ m s}^{-1}$
