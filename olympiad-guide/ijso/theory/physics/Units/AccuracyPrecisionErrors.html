Accuracy, Precision, and Experimental Errors

1. The Core Distinction: Accuracy vs. Precision

These two terms describe the "quality" of your data from two different angles.

  • Accuracy: This is about Truth. How close is your average measurement to the actual, accepted value?
  • Precision: This is about Consistency. How close are your repeated measurements to each other?

The Dartboard Analogy

  • High Accuracy, Low Precision: Your darts are all over the board, but their "average" position is the bullseye.
  • Low Accuracy, High Precision: All your darts are tightly bunched together in the top-right corner. You are consistent, but consistently wrong.
  • High Accuracy, High Precision: The gold standard. All darts are bunched tightly in the bullseye.

2. Categorizing Experimental Errors

We classify errors based on their source and how they affect our data.

A. Systematic Errors (The "Bias" Error)

These errors push all your readings in the same direction (always too high or always too low). They ruin your Accuracy.

  • Zero Error: A voltmeter that reads $0.1\text{ V}$ when nothing is connected.
  • Calibration Error: A wooden ruler that has expanded or shrunk due to humidity.
  • Parallax Error: A consistent habit of reading a scale from the wrong angle.

The Fix: You cannot fix these by taking averages. You must calibrate your equipment or apply a mathematical correction factor to every reading.

B. Random Errors (The "Noise" Error)

These are unpredictable fluctuations that shift readings in both directions. They ruin your Precision.

  • Causes: Human reaction time with a stopwatch, slight air currents on a digital balance, or temperature "noise" in a circuit.

The Fix: Take multiple readings and calculate the mean. Random errors tend to cancel each other out when averaged.

3. The Math of Uncertainty

In your STEM guides and IJSO practicals, you’ll need to quantify these errors using three main methods:

Absolute Error ($\Delta x$)

The raw difference between your measurement ($x_m$) and the true value ($x_t$).

$\Delta x = |x_m - x_t|$

Relative Error

Shows the "scale" of the error relative to the total measurement.

\text{Relative Error} = \frac{\Delta x}{x_t}

Percentage Error

The most common way to report results in a lab report.

\text{Percentage Error} = \frac{|x_m - x_t|}{x_t} \times 100\%

4. Significant Figures: The Language of Precision

Significant figures (Sig Figs) aren't just a math rule; they tell the reader exactly how sensitive your measuring tool was.

Rule Example Sig Figs
All non-zero digits count$1.25$3
"Sandwiched" zeros count$105.02$5
Leading zeros never count$0.002$1
Trailing zeros (with a decimal) count$2.50$3

Critical Calculation Rule: Your final answer can never be more precise than your least precise measurement. If you multiply $2.5$ (2 sig figs) by $3.1415$ (5 sig figs), your answer must be rounded to 2 sig figs.

💡 Lab Strategy for Competitive Physics

  • Identify the Limiting Factor: Is your experiment limited by your own reaction time (random) or a faulty scale (systematic)?
  • The Power of 5: For IJSO-level practicals, always aim for at least 5 sets of readings for any variable to minimize random error.
  • Eye Level is Key: When reading a meniscus (the curve of a liquid), always read from the bottom of the curve at eye level to avoid parallax.
