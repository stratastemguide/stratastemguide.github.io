1. Why Units are Non-Negotiable

Think of a unit as the "identity" of a number. Saying "the distance is 170" is physically meaningless. In a lab, 170 grams (a small box) vs. 170 kilograms (a heavy metal cube) is the difference between a successful experiment and a broken workbench.

The International System (SI) was built to stop this confusion by providing a universal "source of truth."

3. Derived Units: The Combinations

Most quantities you’ll use in mechanics or electronics are "Derived." This means they are products or ratios of the base units.

  • Velocity: $\text{Distance} \div \text{Time} = \text{m/s}$ (or $\text{m}\cdot\text{s}^{-1}$)
  • Acceleration: $\text{Velocity} \div \text{Time} = \text{m/s}^2$ (or $\text{m}\cdot\text{s}^{-2}$)

4. SI Prefixes: Handling the Extremes

Physics deals with the massive (galaxies) and the microscopic (atoms). Prefixes allow us to keep the numbers manageable.

Nano (n): $10^{-9}$ (Atomic scales)

Micro ($\mu$): $10^{-6}$ (Biological cells)

Milli (m): $10^{-3}$ (Standard small measurements)

Kilo (k): $10^{3}$ (Large scale)

Mega (M): $10^{6}$ (Power grids)

Giga (G): $10^{9}$ (Data/Frequencies)

5. The Conversion Trap: Area and Volume

The biggest mistake students make is forgetting that when you square or cube a unit, you must also square or cube the conversion factor.

Example: Converting $1 \text{ m}^2$ to $\text{cm}^2$

We know $1 \text{ m} = 100 \text{ cm}$.
Therefore, $1 \text{ m}^2 = (100 \text{ cm}) \times (100 \text{ cm}) = 10,000 \text{ cm}^2$.
Not $100 \text{ cm}^2$!

Volume Insight:

  • $1 \text{ m}^3 = 1,000,000 \text{ cm}^3$
  • $1 \text{ dm}^3 = 1 \text{ Liter}$
  • $1 \text{ cm}^3 = 1 \text{ mL}$

6. Astronomical Units (Large Scale Physics)

In IJSO-level physics, you'll often step outside the lab and into space. You need to recognize these "specialty" units:

  • Astronomical Unit (AU): $\approx 1.5 \times 10^8 \text{ km}$ (Earth to Sun distance).
  • Light Year (ly): $\approx 9.5 \times 10^{12} \text{ km}$ (Distance light travels in a year).
  • Parsec (pc): $\approx 3.1 \times 10^{13} \text{ km}$ (Used for even deeper space).

💡 Pro-Tip for Problem Solving

Treat units like algebraic variables. If you are calculating Speed ($v = d/t$) and you divide meters by seconds, your unit must be m/s. If your units don't cancel out to the expected final unit, you've likely made an algebraic error.
