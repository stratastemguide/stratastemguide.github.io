1. Defining Dimensions: The Physics "Data Type"

While units like meters, feet, or inches describe the scale, Dimensions describe the fundamental nature of a quantity. In physics, we treat dimensions as a qualitative description.

Notation: We always place the quantity inside square brackets $[ ]$ to show we are discussing its dimension, not its value.

The Big Three: Most mechanics problems only require $[M]$ (Mass), $[L]$ (Length), and $[T]$ (Time).

Why this is your best tool in an exam:

  • Consistency Check: You can verify if a complex equation is even possible. Both sides must have the same dimensions.
  • Conversion Factor Discovery: It helps you find how one unit relates to another.
  • Formula Derivation: If you know what factors a variable depends on, you can "build" the formula without memorizing it.

2. The 7 Fundamental SI Dimensions

Every physical quantity, no matter how complex, is just a combination of these seven "base types."

3. Deep Dive: Derived Dimensions

To master this, you must be able to break down common variables into their base dimensions quickly.

Area

$[L] \times [L] = \color{#22c55e}{[L^2]}$

Volume

$[L] \times [L] \times [L] = \color{#0ea5e9}{[L^3]}$

Velocity

$\frac{\text{Length}}{\text{Time}} = \color{#a855f7}{[LT^{-1}]}$

Acceleration

$\frac{\text{Velocity}}{\text{Time}} = \color{#f97316}{[LT^{-2}]}$

Force (Mass × Accel)

$[M] \times [LT^{-2}] = \color{#ef4444}{[MLT^{-2}]}$

Work/Energy (Force × Dist)

$[MLT^{-2}] \times [L] = \color{#eab308}{[ML^2T^{-2}]}$

4. Advanced Application: The Method of Dimensions

This is a favorite for IJSO theory papers. Let's look at how to derive a relationship from scratch.

Scenario

The period ($T$) of a simple pendulum depends on the mass of the bob ($m$), the length of the string ($l$), and the acceleration due to gravity ($g$).

  1. Step 1: Set up the proportionality

    $T \propto m^a l^b g^c$

    $T = k \cdot m^a l^b g^c$ (where $k$ is a dimensionless constant)

  2. Step 2: Write the dimensions for each side
    • Period ($T$): $[M^0 L^0 T^1]$
    • Mass ($m$): $[M^1 L^0 T^0]$
    • Length ($l$): $[M^0 L^1 T^0]$
    • Gravity ($g$): $[M^0 L^1 T^{-2}]$
  3. Step 3: Equate the powers

    $[T^1] = [M]^a [L]^b [LT^{-2}]^c$

    $[T^1] = M^a L^{b+c} T^{-2c}$

    • For $M$: $a = 0$ (This proves the period doesn't depend on mass!)
    • For $T$: $-2c = 1 \rightarrow c = -1/2$
    • For $L$: $b + c = 0 \rightarrow b - 1/2 = 0 \rightarrow b = 1/2$
  4. Step 4: Final Formula

    $T = k \cdot m^0 l^{1/2} g^{-1/2} \Rightarrow T = k \sqrt{\frac{l}{g}}$

5. The "Silent" Rules & Limitations

Dimensional analysis is powerful, but it has boundaries you must remember for your STEM guides:

  • The Constant Problem: You cannot find the value of dimensionless constants (like the $2\pi$ in the pendulum formula) using this method.
  • Function Blindness: Dimensions cannot handle logarithmic, exponential, or trigonometric functions. For example, in $\sin(\theta)$, the $\theta$ must be dimensionless.
  • Vector Blindness: Dimensions don't tell you if a quantity is a scalar or a vector.
  • The Addition Rule: You can only add or subtract terms with the same dimensions. This is called the Principle of Homogeneity.
